## Section 1 — Hook & What is PITL (0:00–0:45)

**On screen (optional):** Title card — “PITL: Persona In The Loop”

**Script:**

> “What if an AI assistant could match not just your topic, but your *level*—and switch between supporting you where you are and gently pushing you to learn more?
>
> That’s the idea behind **PITL**—**Persona In The Loop**. PITL is a research platform for **competency-based persona matching** in human–AI collaboration. It’s designed so that learners and developers work with AI personas that fit their expertise and that can **adapt** over time.
>
> In this short video I’ll walk you through the main ideas and how the system works.”

---

## Section 2 — The Problem & Dual Mode (0:45–1:35)

**On screen (optional):** Two columns — “Similar” | “Complementary”

**Script:**

> “Most AI tools give the same kind of response to everyone. They don’t really adapt to whether you’re a beginner or an expert, or whether you need to get something done quickly or you want to learn.
>
> PITL uses **two modes** instead of one.
>
> In **Similar mode**, the AI persona is close to your own profile—same domain, similar level. That keeps cognitive load lower and helps you work efficiently. Think of it as staying in your comfort zone.
>
> In **Complementary mode**, the persona complements your profile—it fills gaps and stretches you a bit. That supports learning and skill growth, like working in the zone of proximal development.
>
> The system doesn’t lock you into one mode. It can **switch** between Similar and Complementary based on your workload and learning goals. That’s the core of the dual-mode design.”

---

## Section 3 — How It Works: User Journey (1:35–2:35)

**On screen (optional):** Simple flow: Consent → Competency → Tasks → Feedback

**Script:**

> “Here’s how a user experiences PITL.
>
> First, they give **informed consent** and then go through a **competency assessment**. We use a questionnaire inspired by the Dreyfus model, so we capture both technical and pedagogical strengths. From that we build a **user profile**—a compact representation of their level and domain.
>
> The system then picks **two personas** from a pool of a hundred: one **Similar** and one **Complementary** to that profile. Each persona has a clear role, style, and expertise level—so the AI doesn’t feel generic.
>
> Next come **tasks**—for example, small blockchain or smart contract exercises. For each task, the user works with either the Similar or the Complementary persona. They get a prompt, the AI generates code or explanations in line with that persona, and they can compare how the two modes feel.
>
> After each task we collect **NASA-TLX** ratings—a standard way to measure mental demand and cognitive load. That feedback is what allows the system to **adapt**: if load is too high, it can lean toward Similar; if the user is ready for more, it can lean toward Complementary.”

---

## Section 4 — Personas & Adaptive Switching (2:35–3:35)

**On screen (optional):** “100 Personas = 2 domains × 10 subdomains × 5 Dreyfus levels”

**Script:**

> “The persona pool is structured in a simple way: **two main domains**—technology and education—each with **several subdomains**, and each subdomain has personas at **five Dreyfus levels**, from novice to expert. So we have many distinct personas, not one generic assistant.
>
> When we say the system is **adaptive**, we mean it uses the NASA-TLX scores and some simple rules to decide which mode to offer next. If cognitive load has been high, the next task might use Similar mode to reduce pressure. If load is manageable and we want to support learning, we might switch to Complementary. There’s also a **diversity** rule so that over a session users experience both modes. So the same person might get a different sequence than someone else—that’s the adaptation.
>
> In our studies we compared this **adaptive** setup to a **fixed** sequence—always the same order of Similar and Complementary. We found that the adaptive condition led to better learning gains and code quality on average, across different expertise levels. So the adaptation isn’t just a feature; it seems to help.”

---

## Section 5 — Context & Main Findings (3:35–4:25)

**On screen (optional):** “Blockchain education • Research results (H1–H4)”

**Script:**

> “PITL is implemented in the context of **blockchain and smart contract education**—so the tasks and personas are geared toward that. But the idea is broader: any domain where you have both technical and pedagogical dimensions could use a similar dual-mode, competency-based setup.
>
> Our main results line up with the design. **Complementary mode** tended to produce higher learning gains; **Similar mode** tended to produce lower cognitive load and sometimes higher perceived code quality. So we see a trade-off: one mode for learning, one for efficiency. The **Mod-by-Dreyfus interaction** was significant for cognitive load and task time—so expertise level really does change how heavy or long the two modes feel. And as I said, **adaptive** switching beat a fixed sequence on learning and quality. We also did interviews; users often said Complementary felt more ‘teaching-oriented’ and Similar more ‘smooth and efficient,’ which matches the numbers.”

It’s built for research on human–AI collaboration and learning, with a focus on blockchain education.
>

## Tips for recording

1. **Pace:** Speak clearly and a bit slower than normal; ~140–150 words per minute keeps 5 minutes.
2. **Visuals:** Show the platform (consent → competency → one task with Similar/Complementary, NASA-TLX) or use 2–3 slides (problem → dual mode → flow).
3. **One idea per sentence:** Short sentences are easier to follow in a short video.
4. **Emphasize:** “PITL,” “Similar,” “Complementary,” “adaptive,” “cognitive load,” “Dreyfus.”
5. **Optional:** Add 5–10 seconds of intro/outro music; trim Section 2 or 5 if you run over.
